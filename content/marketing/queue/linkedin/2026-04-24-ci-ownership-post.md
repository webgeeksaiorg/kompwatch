The most important question before buying a competitive intelligence tool:

**Who at your company will own it?**

Not "who will use it." Who will *own* it. Who will triage the alerts, decide what's noise, update the battlecards, and make sure insights actually reach the people who need them.

I've talked to teams who bought Crayon or Klue and got real value. Every single one had a dedicated person — a PM, a CI analyst, sometimes a product marketer — who spent 8–15 hours/week on the workflow. That's not optional. That's the product.

I've also talked to teams who bought the same tools and hit month three with a $28K line item and nothing to show for it. No owner. The alerts flooded in and nobody acted on them.

The tool didn't fail. The org design failed.

Three patterns I've seen work for smaller teams (under 50 people):

1. **Scope it down.** Track 5–8 pages per competitor, not the full internet. Pricing, features, jobs, homepage. Get a daily summary, not real-time pings.

2. **Give one person 30 minutes a week.** Not a full role. Just one PM or founder who reads the digest and flags anything relevant.

3. **Match the tool to the owner you have.** Don't buy a $28K platform for a 30-min/week problem.

The expensive tools aren't overpriced for what they do. They're overpriced for what most teams will *actually do with them*.
