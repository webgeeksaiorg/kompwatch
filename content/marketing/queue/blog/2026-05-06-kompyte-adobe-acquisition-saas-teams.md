# Kompyte Is Now Adobe Property. Here's What That Means If You're a SaaS Team.

Adobe closed the Semrush acquisition on April 28, 2026. $1.9B.

Kompyte — the competitive intelligence platform Semrush bought in March 2022 — is now three acquisitions deep inside Adobe's enterprise portfolio. Kompyte → Semrush → Adobe.

I want to be direct about what this means, because most of the coverage so far is focused on Adobe's stock price and what this does for the Experience Cloud. None of it is written for the SaaS PM who just wants to know when a competitor changes their pricing page.

## Why Adobe Bought Semrush (It Wasn't for CI)

Adobe's press release uses the phrases "AI-driven brand visibility," "digital discovery," and "customer experience intelligence." The pitch is about helping Fortune 500 marketing teams track brand presence across AI search, social, and digital channels.

That's a real market. It's just not your market.

Kompyte represents a small slice of what Semrush sold. Competitive intelligence for B2B SaaS teams is not what drove a $1.9B acquisition. Adobe bought Semrush for the SEO data, the brand monitoring, and the enterprise CX story. Kompyte came along.

This matters because product roadmaps follow strategic priorities. When a tool gets acquired — especially as a portfolio addition rather than a core rationale — the roadmap goes into a queue while teams integrate, align incentives, and figure out what gets resourced.

12 to 24 months of slower development is the realistic expectation after an acquisition like this. Not zero progress. Slower.

## What Kompyte Was Already Like Before This

Let's be fair: the Adobe acquisition adds uncertainty on top of pre-existing issues that Kompyte users were already working around.

**The pricing model.** Kompyte's listed starting price is around $300/month. When Semrush acquired Kompyte in 2022, the acquisition filing disclosed an average ARR per customer of roughly $20,000. That's $1,667/month actual average.

The gap between "starting at" and "what you pay" is where upsell conversations happen. Kompyte's pricing scales by competitor and by user seat. Tracking your 11th competitor requires a sales call. That's the model.

**AI features that underdeliver.** G2 and Gartner Peer Insights reviews from 2025–2026 consistently flag this. The AI-generated battlecard layer sounds good in demos. In practice, reviewers note data depth gaps and features that feel more like roadmap promises than shipped capabilities.

**No export to PowerPoint or Excel.** This comes up repeatedly. Battlecards live inside Kompyte. Getting that data out requires manual copy-paste. For a tool at $20K ARR, that's a real gap.

**Occasionally outdated information.** The monitoring is not real-time. Reviews note delays in surfacing competitor changes, particularly for website content (not just press releases and social).

None of this means Kompyte doesn't work. Plenty of teams use it and get value. But the realistic picture is: a tool with genuine friction points, at enterprise pricing, now inside a company whose priorities are elsewhere.

## What Your Options Are Now

### Stay on Kompyte

This is a valid choice, especially if it's working for you and you're mid-contract. The acquisition doesn't break anything immediately. Support continues. The product runs.

The risk is 12–24 months of slower feature development and an integration roadmap driven by Adobe's priorities, not yours.

If you're up for renewal in the next 6 months, run the evaluation now, before the pressure hits.

### Go to Klue or Crayon

If you need enterprise-grade sales enablement — AI battlecards that sync to Salesforce, rep enablement workflows, competitive win/loss integration — Klue ($16–30K/yr) and Crayon ($20–100K+/yr) are the clear alternatives.

Neither is dramatically different from Kompyte in price tier. Both require a dedicated owner (their own implementation guides say 7–8 weeks onboarding before you see value, and a CI analyst to keep the content fresh).

If that's your situation, one of those makes sense.

### Use a purpose-built website monitoring tool

There's a different question buried in a lot of Kompyte evaluations: *do you actually need battlecards, or do you need to know when your competitor changes something?*

Those are different problems.

Kompyte (and Klue, and Crayon) are built around the battlecard workflow — curated competitive intelligence that sales reps can pull up before a call. It's a real use case. It requires analyst time to keep current.

## The Question Worth Asking Before You Renew

Most teams I talk to use Kompyte (or a competitor) for 20% of what it can do. Pricing page tracking, homepage copy changes, new feature announcements. The battlecard layer is either unmaintained or owned by one person who just exports it into a Notion doc.

If that's your situation — you're paying $1,667/month for what's functionally a website change alert — the Adobe acquisition is the moment to ask whether that's the right tool.
