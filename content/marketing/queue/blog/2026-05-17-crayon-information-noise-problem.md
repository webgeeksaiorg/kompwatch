# The Crayon Information Noise Problem: Why Sales Teams Stop Using It

Crayon doesn't fail because it doesn't have enough data.

It fails because it has too much.

The product does exactly what it promises. It monitors competitors across hundreds of data sources — website changes, job listings, press releases, social posts, review sites, pricing pages, app store updates, patent filings. It aggregates everything into a feed. It sends you a digest.

The digest is enormous.

---

## The inbox problem

I've talked to several ex-Crayon users in the past few months — PMs, revenue ops leads, product marketers. The story is consistent.

Week one: excited. Dozens of competitor signals landing in the inbox. "We're actually on top of this now."

Week three: the volume is real but management is starting to feel like a second job. The digest has 40+ items. Everything has the same visual weight. A competitor's new job listing and a competitor's pricing page change sit side by side with identical formatting.

Week eight: most of the team has inbox filters set to skip the digest. The one person still reading it is the PMM who set it up. They're summarizing manually and pasting into Slack every Friday.

Week sixteen: the renewal conversation happens and someone asks "is anyone actually using this?"

This isn't a Crayon criticism exactly. It's a description of what happens when you collect everything and score nothing.

---

## The signal weighting problem

A competitive intelligence feed without severity weighting is just a feed.

Here's what Crayon might send you in a single daily digest:

1. Competitor A posted a new job listing for a Sales Development Rep
2. Competitor B was mentioned in a SaaStr podcast episode
3. Competitor C changed their pricing page
4. Competitor A published a blog post about their product roadmap
5. Competitor B's Glassdoor rating dropped from 3.8 to 3.6
6. Competitor C updated their homepage headline

Items 1, 2, 4, and 5: mildly interesting background noise. You probably don't need to act on any of them this week.

Item 3: your most direct competitor just changed their pricing. That affects your next 10 sales calls. You need to know what changed, whether they went up or down, and whether they adjusted their feature tiers.

Item 6: your most direct competitor just changed their positioning headline. That's a messaging strategy shift. Worth reviewing your own battlecard this week.

Those are very different urgency levels. If your tool sends them all at equal weight — and Crayon largely does by default — your team learns that most signals are noise. And then they start treating all signals as noise.

This is exactly the adoption problem. Gartner keeps reporting 31% rep adoption of CI tools. This is part of why. Not bad tools — bad signal-to-noise.

## What "information overload" actually costs

The number I keep coming back to: 31% rep adoption for enterprise CI tools.

These are tools companies pay $20K–$100K a year for. Three out of ten reps engage with the output consistently. The other seven aren't ignoring the tool because they don't care about competitive intel. They're ignoring it because the tool trained them that checking is usually unrewarding.

Competitive intelligence with 31% adoption is just another line item your finance team will question at renewal.

The math becomes obvious when you frame it that way. You can have comprehensive data coverage, or you can have rep behavior change. The tools that chase the first often sacrifice the second.

## The practical question

Before your next CI tool renewal, try this: ask three sales reps when they last looked at a competitive intelligence alert and what they did with it.

If the answer is "I have those filtered" or "I skim it sometimes but most of it isn't relevant to my deals" — you have the noise problem.

The fix isn't a better digest template. It's a tool that scores signals and surfaces only the ones that matter to active deals. That's a design decision upstream of the digest format.

## FAQ

**Is Crayon actually bad?**
No. It's a genuinely powerful CI platform for companies with a dedicated analyst or PMM who manages it. The problem is the sales teams the tool is supposed to serve — they're downstream of someone filtering the signal manually. That's fine at $100K contracts. It's hard to justify at any tier where there's nobody whose job is filtering.

**Does KompWatch replace Crayon?**
Different tier, different buyer. Crayon is for companies with CI headcount and analyst workflow. KompWatch is for teams that need to know when their competitor's pricing page changed. They're not the same product.

**What's the minimum setup that actually works?**
3–5 competitors. 2–3 pages each: pricing, features/product, and homepage hero. Check frequency: every 6 hours. Severity threshold: only alert on changes that score above 30. That keeps the weekly digest under 10 items and all of them meaningful.
