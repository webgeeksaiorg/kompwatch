# Free Competitive Battlecard Template (Copy-Paste Ready)

A competitive battlecard is a one-page cheat sheet for your sales team. Use it before a call when a competitor comes up. The goal isn't to memorize everything — it's to have the right things ready.

Here's a template you can copy. Fill in the blanks for each competitor you care about. Keep it under one page. If you can't fit it on one page, it's too much.

---

## The Template

---

**[COMPETITOR NAME] Battlecard**  
*Last updated: [DATE] — review monthly*

### Who They Are
One or two sentences. What do they actually do? Who's the core customer?

> *Example: Crayon is an enterprise competitive intelligence platform for large GTM teams. Median contract $28,750/year. Requires a dedicated CI analyst to get full value.*

### When We Win

## How to Use This Template

Fill it out for your top 3–5 competitors. Not all of them. Focus on the ones that come up on actual sales calls.

Share it with your sales team in Notion, a shared Google Doc, or wherever they live. A battlecard nobody can find is the same as no battlecard.

**The part most teams skip:** keeping it current.

Pricing changes constantly. Features get added and removed. Free tiers disappear. If your battlecard has pricing data that's 8 months old, a prospect will catch it, and your rep's credibility takes a hit.

The easy way to catch this: set up a competitor monitoring tool to alert you when their pricing page changes. Then review and update the relevant battlecard section. Takes 5 minutes when you get the alert instead of an audit every quarter.

## FAQ

**How often should I update my battlecard?**  
At minimum, quarterly. In practice: update the pricing and features sections whenever your monitoring tool pings you. The positioning sections change slower — review those quarterly.

**How many competitors should I build battlecards for?**  
Start with 3. The ones that actually come up on sales calls. Broad coverage sounds good but in practice your team won't remember 12 battlecards. Three crisp ones beat twelve stale ones.

**Should battlecards be public or internal?**  
Internal. You're sharing your weaknesses and competitive strategy. Don't let competitors read it.

**What's the difference between a battlecard and a comparison page?**  
Your comparison page (/compare, /vs-competitor) is for prospects to read. Your battlecard is for your sales team. Different audiences, different tone. Comparison pages can be diplomatic. Battlecards should be blunt.
