# What Happened to Kompyte? (And What to Use Instead)

If you're searching for Kompyte and landing on Semrush pages, or getting emails about an "Adobe Digital Experience" transition — you're not confused. Kompyte is effectively gone. Here's the full timeline.

---

## The Ownership Chain

**2022:** Semrush acquires Kompyte for an undisclosed amount. The pitch: Kompyte's competitive intelligence layer would complement Semrush's SEO data. Product stays roughly the same, branding starts to blur.

**November 2025:** Adobe announces it's acquiring Semrush for $1.9 billion.

**April 2026:** Deal closes. Semrush — and everything inside it, including what used to be Kompyte — is now part of Adobe's Digital Experience suite. Same portfolio as Marketo, Workfront, and Adobe Analytics.

If you were a Kompyte customer four years ago, your tool has changed ownership twice and now lives inside one of the largest enterprise software stacks on the planet.

---

## What This Means in Practice

Adobe's target customer is an enterprise marketing team with a six-figure budget and a dedicated CI analyst. If that's you, the Semrush acquisition might be fine. You get more integrations, more data, and a vendor that isn't going anywhere.

If you're a 10-person SaaS company who just wanted to know when a competitor changed their pricing page — you're in trouble. Adobe doesn't sell to you. Their sales motion starts at "book a demo" and ends at "here's your custom enterprise contract."

Kompyte's original value prop was accessibility. That's gone.

---

## The Broader Problem

Kompyte isn't alone. The whole CI tool market moved upmarket at the same time.

**Crayon** was acquired by SoftwareOne (July 2025), rebranded under the SoftwareOne name (January 2026). SoftwareOne is a $3B+ enterprise software licensing company. Crayon's standalone CI product is now a feature inside an enterprise sales bundle.

**Klue** raised $62M Series B in January 2026, then acquired Ignition, Goldpan.ai, and DoubleCheck Research in five months. When a company raises $60M and immediately buys three tools, they're building an enterprise platform — not improving the core product for the SMB customers who got them to Series B.

The pattern is obvious. Every tool that was "affordable for a startup" is now inside a $1B+ corporate stack or raising enough money that SMB is no longer the target.

---

## What Actually Works for Small Teams Now

If you're a small SaaS team, your real options are:

**Google Alerts** — Free. Only catches indexed content (press releases, blog posts). Won't catch a pricing change, a rewritten features page, or a removed free tier. Still worth having, but not sufficient.

**Visualping** — Web monitoring tool. Good for basic page change detection. No AI summarization, no competitive context, no change categorization. You'll get an alert that "something changed" and then need to figure out what and why.

**DIY cron job** — A Python script that diffs HTML pages and emails you changes. Works. Breaks on SPAs and JavaScript-heavy sites. You'll spend a weekend building it and then maintain it forever.

**KompWatch** — Built specifically for this gap after spending years doing the DIY cron job approach. Paste competitor URLs, it takes hourly snapshots, AI explains changes in plain English. Handles SPAs. $49/month for 10 competitors, $149/month for 50. No sales call. No onboarding ceremony.

I'm biased (I built it), but the honest pitch is: it's the tool I wanted when I was manually checking 12 competitor websites every Monday morning.

## Should You Wait for Kompyte to Come Back?

No. It's not coming back. The Kompyte brand is dead — what exists now is a feature inside Semrush's platform, which is a feature inside Adobe's enterprise suite. There's no roadmap for a standalone SMB-focused Kompyte product.

If you're on a legacy Kompyte contract, worth asking your rep directly what the migration path is. Don't expect it to be cheap or simple.

## FAQ

**Is Kompyte still available?**
Technically yes — it exists as part of Semrush, which is now owned by Adobe. But as a standalone product for SMBs, it's effectively discontinued. Pricing and availability are now part of Adobe's enterprise sales process.

**What did Kompyte do?**
Competitive intelligence monitoring — track competitor websites, pricing, job listings, news mentions. Crayon and Klue do similar things at the enterprise level. KompWatch does the website tracking piece at the startup/SMB level.
