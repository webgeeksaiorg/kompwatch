# Kompyte After Semrush: What Small Teams Are Doing Now

Kompyte used to be the obvious answer when someone asked "what do you use to track competitors without paying Crayon prices?"

$300/year. Set it up in an afternoon. Website monitoring, change alerts, some basic battlecard features. Good enough for a 10-person startup that doesn't have a dedicated competitive intelligence analyst.

Then Semrush acquired them.

That was late 2022. Three and a half years ago. Long enough to see what an acquisition actually does to a product.

---

## What changed after the acquisition

Kompyte didn't disappear. But it changed direction.

Semrush is an $800M/year enterprise software company. Their roadmap is optimized for enterprise buyers — large marketing teams, agencies, companies with multiple product lines and dedicated research staff. When Kompyte became part of that machine, the feature priorities shifted accordingly.

The small team features — the ones that made Kompyte worth $300/year — stopped getting the same attention. New features serve the Semrush integration story. The roadmap is now "how does Kompyte fit into the Semrush platform" rather than "what does a 5-person SaaS team actually need to track competitors."

This isn't unusual. It's almost always what happens. The acquisition is great for the founders. For the product's original users, it's a coin flip.

---

## The actual user complaints

This isn't speculation. Here's what comes up consistently in reviews posted after 2023:

**Feature velocity slowed.** Things that were "on the roadmap" two years ago still aren't shipped. Enterprise integrations get built; SMB workflows don't.

**Pricing crept up.** The $300/year entry tier is still there, technically. But the features you actually want — better AI summaries, more granular alerts, deeper integrations — are now in higher tiers that push toward $100+/month.

**Support response times changed.** When Kompyte was independent, support was fast. Post-acquisition, it moved through Semrush's support structure. Response times lengthened. Small team issues get lower priority than enterprise tickets.

**The product "fit" shifted.** Kompyte's UI and workflow feel optimized for someone who also uses Semrush's other tools. If you're not a Semrush user, the experience is more disjointed than it used to be.

None of this makes Kompyte a bad product. It just makes it a different product than the one that originally attracted indie SaaS teams.

---

## What teams are switching to

A few patterns emerge from the people actively looking for Kompyte alternatives:

**Option 1: DIY (Visualping + Google Alerts + spreadsheet)**

Visualping has ~10,000 active website monitors. It's free for basic use. Google Alerts covers press mentions. A spreadsheet ties it together.

This works if someone owns the process. It breaks when nobody does. The "someone" usually turns out to be whoever is most anxious about competitive threats, which means it runs inconsistently.

The output is also raw: "the pricing page changed." Not "they added a new annual discount tier targeted at mid-market." You're looking at diffs, not meaning.

**Option 2: Crayon or Klue**

These are the enterprise options. Crayon starts around $15K/year. Klue is similar.

They're genuinely good products for the teams they're designed for — enterprise sales orgs with dedicated enablement staff, companies that need battlecard automation at scale.

For a 10-person startup? You're paying for features you'll never use, and you'll probably end up as one of the 69% of teams where the reps stop logging in after the first month.

**Option 3: Purpose-built tools for small teams**

This is the gap that's been underserved: teams that need real monitoring (not manual), AI-powered summaries (not raw HTML diffs), push delivery (not a portal you have to remember to check), at a price that makes sense before Series B.

That's exactly what I built KompWatch for. I was one of the Kompyte users. I had six competitor tabs pinned. I forgot to check half of them. The other half I checked but didn't have time to synthesize what I saw.
