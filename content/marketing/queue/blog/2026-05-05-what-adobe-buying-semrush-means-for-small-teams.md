# What Adobe Buying Semrush Means If You're Not a Fortune 500

Adobe just paid $1.9B for Semrush. Which means Adobe now owns Kompyte.

If you're a Kompyte customer, let me tell you what happens next — because I've watched this movie before.

## The acquisition playbook

When a big enterprise platform acquires a tool you depend on, the sequence is predictable:

**Year 1:** Nothing changes. They say the roadmap is "intact." Support tickets get a little slower.

**Year 2:** Pricing "adjusts." The standalone product gets bundled into the acquirer's suite. The cheaper tiers quietly disappear.

**Year 3:** They sunset the standalone product entirely or reposition it as an enterprise module at 3x the price.

Kompyte was already Kompyte by Semrush. Now it's Kompyte by Semrush by Adobe. Two acquisitions deep inside a $1.9B enterprise deal whose core strategy is competing with Salesforce and HubSpot in marketing intelligence.

Small SaaS teams tracking competitor pricing pages? Not exactly the ICP Adobe optimized for.

## Who this actually affects

If you're at a 200+ person company with a CI team and an enterprise contract, you're probably fine. Adobe keeps the enterprise customers. The contracts are big enough to matter.

If you're a 10–50 person SaaS team using Kompyte because it was the most accessible mid-market option — start looking now. Not because Kompyte will disappear tomorrow, but because the 12–24 month integration period is when prioritization decisions get made. You want to be off the platform before your needs become someone else's backlog item.

## The broader pattern

Crayon raised venture money and went upmarket. Klue is $16–30K/year and launched an enterprise "Compete Agent" product. Kompyte got acquired twice.

Every competitor monitoring tool that started in the accessible mid-market eventually moved upmarket. The VC math requires it.

What that leaves is a gap: teams under 100 people who need to monitor 5–15 competitor websites, get plain-English summaries of what changed, and don't want to hire a CI analyst or sign a $25K contract.

That gap is real. It's also largely unserved right now.

## The practical alternative

Here's what the DIY stack looks like for most teams once they realize enterprise tools aren't the answer:

- **Visualping** (free tier): 5 pages, raw HTML diffs, misses JavaScript-rendered content
- **Google Alerts**: catches news *about* competitors, completely misses website changes
- **Manual Monday tab-check**: works until you miss something on a Wednesday

The problem with the DIY stack isn't effort — it's coverage and reliability. You can't monitor 10 competitors across 3 pages each with Visualping's free tier and a Monday morning ritual. Things slip through.

## What to look for instead

When evaluating alternatives, a few things actually matter:

**Headless browser, not raw HTML.** Most SaaS pricing pages are JavaScript-rendered. A tool that fetches raw HTML sees an empty div. You need something that runs a real browser. (This is the thing Visualping gets wrong for modern sites.)

**Plain English output.** You don't want raw diffs. You want "Competitor X added a $99/month tier between Pro and Enterprise." That's what AI summaries do when they work well.

**No analyst required.** The Crayon/Klue model assumes you have someone to synthesize data. If you don't have that person, the platform becomes expensive shelfware within six months. (Not speculation — this is what we found in every conversation with teams that churned off those platforms.)

**Pricing you can justify.** The math on $15–30K/year only works if CI is a core function with dedicated headcount. For most teams it's not. $49/month is something a product manager can expense.

## FAQ

**Is Kompyte going away?**
Not immediately. But Adobe's strategic focus is enterprise marketing intelligence. Kompyte's small-team features are unlikely to be a priority during integration.

**What's the fastest way to switch?**
Export your competitor list and tracked URLs from Kompyte (do this now, before any migration). Most URL-based monitoring tools accept the same inputs.

**Do I need a headless browser tool or will raw HTML work?**
Depends on what you're monitoring. Legacy static pages (old-school WordPress, basic HTML) — raw HTML is fine. Modern SaaS apps where pricing and features are JavaScript-rendered — you need a headless browser or you'll miss changes.

**How often should I be checking competitor pages?**
Daily is the baseline. If you have competitors in active pricing wars or feature races, every 6 hours. Hourly is overkill for most.

**What about free tools?**
Visualping's free tier is 5 page checks. Google Alerts misses website changes entirely (it catches news articles *about* competitors). For 3 competitors and 1 page each, the free stack works. Beyond that it breaks down.
