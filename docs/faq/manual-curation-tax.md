# What Is the Manual Curation Tax in Competitive Intelligence?

Enterprise competitive intelligence platforms like Crayon and Klue don't run themselves. They require a human — usually a dedicated CI manager, product marketing analyst, or competitive intelligence team — to collect, verify, tag, and distribute information before it's useful. This ongoing labor cost is called the **manual curation tax**.

## What Manual Curation Actually Involves

In a typical Crayon or Klue deployment, keeping the platform current requires:

- **Ingesting raw signals** — reviewing AI-surfaced signals from news, social media, review sites, job boards, and web crawls. Not all signals are relevant; a curator decides what matters.
- **Writing and updating battlecards** — translating raw changes into structured competitive positioning documents, reviewing them for accuracy, and pushing updates to the sales team.
- **Tagging and categorizing changes** — assigning context to detected changes (is a pricing change an A/B test or a real move?), so salespeople get clean data rather than noise.
- **Managing false positives** — enterprise CI tools generate a significant volume of low-quality signals. Someone has to review and dismiss them before they reach the sales team.
- **Stakeholder reporting** — preparing weekly or monthly competitive summaries for leadership and product teams, pulling from the curated intelligence base.
- **Onboarding new competitors** — when a new competitor enters the market, someone has to build the initial competitor profile, add relevant pages and keywords, and source comparison data.

For a team running Crayon or Klue at scale, this is a 10–20 hour/week job for at least one person. Larger teams employ a full-time competitive intelligence manager or small CI team.

## What It Costs

A dedicated competitive intelligence analyst or product marketing manager in the US costs $80K–$130K/year in salary, before benefits and overhead. That's on top of the $25K–$40K/year platform cost for Crayon or $20K–$40K/year for Klue.

Total annual spend for a properly staffed enterprise CI function:

| Cost component | Annual cost |
|---|---|
| Platform (Crayon or Klue) | $25,000–$40,000 |
| Dedicated CI analyst (1 FTE) | $85,000–$130,000 |
| **Total** | **$110,000–$170,000+** |

Many early-stage and growth-stage companies try to split CI responsibilities across an existing PM or marketer, resulting in a role that's chronically deprioritized — competitive intelligence becomes a quarterly task instead of a real-time signal.

## Why Enterprise Platforms Require Manual Curation

Enterprise CI platforms are designed around a **human-in-the-loop** model. They surface signals, but rely on a curator to:

1. Determine which signals are material competitive moves (vs. A/B tests, minor copy edits, seasonal rotations)
2. Add strategic context that tools can't supply ("this pricing change matters because they're attacking our mid-market anchor")
3. Translate changes into sales-ready formats (battlecards, talking points, objection handling)
4. Decide which changes to distribute, and to whom

This model makes sense at enterprise scale — where CI feeds a dedicated sales enablement function, and where the quality of battlecard content directly affects $1M+ deal cycles. For smaller teams, the overhead doesn't pencil out.

## When Manual Curation Is Still Worth It

KompWatch is not a substitute for dedicated CI at enterprise scale. Manual curation is worth the investment when:

- You have a large sales team (50+ AEs) where battlecard quality directly affects deal outcomes
- You run complex competitive positioning campaigns that require human strategic judgment
- CI feeds into quarterly board-level reporting and must be tightly curated for executive consumption
- You need a human to own the "competitive narrative" across the company

For these cases, Klue or Crayon — plus a CI analyst — is the right investment. KompWatch is often used as the *monitoring layer* that feeds a Klue or Crayon curator: instead of relying on the platform's own web crawling, a KompWatch alert triggers the curator to investigate.
