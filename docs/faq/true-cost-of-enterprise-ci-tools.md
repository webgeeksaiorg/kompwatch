# What Is the True Cost of Enterprise CI Tools Like Klue and Crayon?

The number in the contract isn't the number you'll actually pay.

Klue and Crayon quote a platform license. They don't quote the analyst, the onboarding time, or the ongoing curator hours required to keep the tool working the way it's supposed to.

---

## The Full Cost Breakdown

| Cost component | Typical range |
|---|---|
| Klue annual contract | $16,000–$40,000/yr |
| Crayon annual contract | $25,000–$50,000/yr |
| Dedicated CI analyst (salary, fully loaded) | $80,000–$110,000/yr |
| Initial onboarding / implementation | 8–15 weeks of internal team time |
| **Effective total (Klue)** | **$96,000–$150,000/yr** |
| **Effective total (Crayon)** | **$105,000–$160,000/yr** |

The license is the entry fee. The analyst is the running cost.

---

## Why You Need an Analyst

Enterprise CI platforms are designed around a human-in-the-loop model. They surface raw signals — website changes, news hits, review activity, social mentions — and rely on a dedicated CI manager to:

- Review and filter signals daily for relevance
- Write and maintain competitive battlecards
- Manage false positives and noise
- Produce stakeholder reports and win/loss analysis
- Onboard new competitors and tune the system over time

This isn't a workaround. It's the intended workflow. Klue's own onboarding process includes a dedicated "CI curator" role by design. The platform is built to support that person, not to replace them.

Without that person:
- Alert volume overwhelms whoever inherits the inbox
- Nobody curates the noise, so quality degrades
- Battlecards go stale
- The tool slowly becomes a dashboard nobody opens

This pattern is common enough that it has a name: **CI shelfware**. See [Why CI Tools Become Shelfware](./why-ci-tools-become-shelfware.md).

---

## The Math That Breaks for Small Teams

At a 200-person company with a real SDR org, the ROI on $140K/year for competitive intelligence can work. One CI analyst keeping 50 reps armed with accurate battlecards, if it wins 2–3 additional deals per year, pays for itself.

At a 10–30 person SaaS, the math usually doesn't work:

- You don't have 50 reps who need battlecards
- You can't justify a $110K analyst headcount for a function you need 10 hours/week
- The license-plus-headcount cost exceeds what the competitive advantage is worth
- Whoever owns it gets pulled in other directions after week 6

This isn't a criticism of Klue or Crayon — they're the right tools for the teams they're designed for. It's a mismatch between the tool's design assumptions and the team's actual structure.

## Questions Worth Asking Before Signing an Enterprise CI Contract

**1. Who specifically owns this tool?**
Name a person, not a team. If no one person is responsible for running it 5–10 hours/week, plan for shelfware.

**2. What's the week-8 workflow?**
Every tool gets used in week 1. The question is what happens in week 8, when the novelty has worn off and everyone's busy. Can you describe the habitual weekly process?

**3. What does the analyst cost, fully loaded?**
Add benefits, equity, management overhead. Don't compare the license price to your current spending — compare license + headcount to your current spending.

**4. What's the cancellation scenario?**
Most enterprise CI contracts are annual. If your CI analyst leaves, you're still paying. Model that risk.
