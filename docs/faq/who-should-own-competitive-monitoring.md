# Who Should Own Competitive Monitoring in Your Team?

Competitive monitoring fails at small companies for one reason more than any other: it becomes a shared responsibility. Shared responsibility is the same as no responsibility. The alerts pile up, nobody feels like it's "theirs" to triage, and within 90 days the tool is open in zero browser tabs.

The fix isn't a better tool. It's assigning one person to own it.

---

## The "Everyone's Job" Problem

When competitive monitoring is distributed — the PM checks it sometimes, marketing has the login too, sales "should be in there" — you get:

- No consistent triage (alerts sit unreviewed for weeks)
- No institutional memory (each person sees fragments, nobody has the full picture)
- No follow-through (a pricing change is noticed but nobody decides what to do with it)
- Gradual disengagement (if the output never connects to decisions, people stop checking)

This isn't a people problem. It's a systems problem. Distributed ownership optimizes for diffused accountability.

---

## The Solution: One Owner

Pick one person. That person receives the digest, triages it, and decides what gets shared with the rest of the team. Digest happens to be a weekly or daily email — low overhead, passive. The owner's job is to scan it, flag what matters, and route it.

At most companies this role naturally lands on one of:

| Role | Why it fits |
|---|---|
| **Founder / CEO** | Closest to strategic decisions; needs the intel for positioning |
| **Product Manager** | Feeding feature roadmap decisions; already owns competitive context |
| **Product Marketing Manager** | Directly owns messaging and positioning; most impacted by competitor moves |
| **Sales Lead** | Needs real-time awareness of pricing changes and new competitor features before demos |
| **Growth Lead** | Tracking messaging shifts and positioning experiments |

Any of these work. The rule: one person, clearly assigned.

## Sharing With the Broader Team

The owner doesn't have to be the only one who reads the intel. Options:

- **Forward digests to a Slack channel or email alias** — team members opt in to the digest as useful context
- **Weekly competitive brief** — the owner picks 1–3 items from the week's digest and writes a quick note to share in a team standup or async channel
- **On-demand sharing** — digests are stored in KompWatch; team members can view change history when they need context for a specific competitor

The distinction: one person makes the digest actionable. Others can consume it. Different roles.

---

## The Minimum Viable Setup for a Small Team

If you're a team of 2–10 and want competitor monitoring to actually work:

1. Decide now who owns it (not "we'll figure it out") — ideally the founder or PM
2. Set up KompWatch with your top 2–3 competitors (Free tier works to start)
3. Have the owner receive the digest to their personal inbox — not a shared alias
4. After 30 days, evaluate: are the digests useful? Are you acting on any of it?

Most teams who fail at this step try to set it up as a shared system before validating that the intel is useful. Start with one person, one inbox, a handful of competitors. Expand when there's evidence it's working.
